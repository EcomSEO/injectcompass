# InjectCompass — Brand Book

The voice, audience, and visual identity for injectcompass.com — the practical-instruction site for injection technique, reconstitution, storage, and supplies.

**Source research:** `docs/injectcompass-research.md` (DataForSEO April 2026). Legal posture: `CLAUDE.md` §Legal positioning.

---

## 1. Who we are

InjectCompass is the patient-education handout you always wished was this clear.

We teach injection technique for people who have been prescribed injectable medications — GLP-1s (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, Zepbound), insulin, and, within a strict research-context framing, peptides published in peer-reviewed literature. We are not a vendor. We do not promote unapproved use. We do not link to research peptide vendors. Ever.

We ship three surfaces that nobody else ships together:

1. **Calculators** — real interactive tools with a visual syringe that highlights the correct tick mark
2. **Technique cards** — step-numbered, printable, video-paired
3. **Troubleshooting decision trees** — bruising, cloudy solution, stuck stopper, missed dose

The tone is nurse-educator, not bro-fitness. The visual language is clinical but warm, not sterile. The line we never cross: suggesting what to inject, only how.

## 2. The reader

### Primary persona — "Rachel, 36, first time injecting a GLP-1"

Just got her first Ozempic pen from the pharmacy. She's a speech therapist in Denver, two kids, husband does SaaS product. Household income $180k-$240k. She's been researching GLP-1s for six months before asking her NP for a prescription.

She's terrified of needles. Not unusually so — normally so. She watched three YouTube videos that contradicted each other. She is reading InjectCompass at 4am because her first dose is tomorrow morning and she can't sleep.

She wants:
- A short video (30-60 seconds, that's it)
- A printable one-page cheat sheet she can tape to the fridge
- The exact needle gauge and length
- A straightforward explanation of why the pen clicks and what the clicks mean
- To know whether she should pinch the skin or not
- To know what to do if she bleeds
- To know that someone real, with a real credential, put this page together

She does not want:
- A 4,000-word explainer from Healthline she has to scroll through
- A vendor's product page disguised as education
- An unverified YouTube tutorial by a bodybuilder
- Reddit, which is great for some things but not for teaching you to inject yourself for the first time

She trusts:
- Nurses (her cousin is one)
- The manufacturer package insert (she already read it)
- Sites that cite where their guidance comes from
- Clear step-numbered procedures

### Secondary persona — "Chris, 42, experienced self-administering"

On tirzepatide for ~18 months. Knows the drill. He's here to:
- Verify reconstitution math on compounded vials (the calculator)
- Check whether a specific type of bruising is normal
- Figure out TSA rules for an upcoming trip
- Confirm the correct needle for a switch from pen to vial

He wants precision and speed. He skips the explainer and goes straight to the calculator or the table. He's 25% of our audience but 50% of our calculator users.

### Tertiary persona — "Aisha, 29, researcher context"

PhD student, reading the peer-reviewed literature on BPC-157, TB-500, or similar. Here for reconstitution math and technique. We serve her within an explicit "research-context only, not for human use" frame — same posture as PepVise. We do not link her to vendors.

### We write for Rachel by default

Chris and Aisha come along for the ride because good beginner content is the best reference content too. But the default voice assumes a scared first-timer, not an expert.

## 3. The promise

> Injections, done right.

Three things every InjectCompass post must deliver:

1. **A step-numbered procedure.** Not prose. A numbered list. Even for a simple technique. Even if it feels redundant.
2. **A printable PDF cheat sheet.** Auto-generated at build from the post content. One page. Refrigerator-ready.
3. **A named citation and a safety call-out.** Every technique post cites the nursing-education literature or the manufacturer package insert. Every technique post has a "Stop and call your prescriber if…" red box where applicable.

## 4. Voice

### How we sound
- **Nurse-educator.** Calm, step-by-step, visual, procedural. Like a really well-written patient-education handout.
- **Precise.** 31-gauge, 5/16 inch, 90-degree angle — not "small needle" or "straight in."
- **Reassuring without promising.** "Most people feel a brief pinch and no more" — not "It's painless!"
- **Safety-aware.** Red boxes for red flags. Amber boxes for cautions. Green boxes for confirmation.
- **Instructional not declarative.** "Hold the pen at a 90-degree angle to your skin" — not "You should be perpendicular."
- **Credentialed-adjacent.** We always cite the nursing-education source or the package insert. We're writing for a reader who would be reassured to see the citation.

### How we don't sound
- ❌ Bro-fitness ("Alright bros, let's get this tirzepatide reconstitution locked in")
- ❌ Vendor-marketing ("Our premium peptide formulations deliver maximum bioavailability")
- ❌ Hype-medical ("This REVOLUTIONARY technique minimizes pain by 90%!")
- ❌ Casually-clinical ("Patients may occasionally experience injection site reactions")
- ❌ Wellness-influencer ("I've been on my GLP-1 journey for 6 months and here's what's worked!")
- ❌ Contrarian ("Forget what the manufacturer says, here's what actually works")

### Side-by-side examples

**❌ Don't write this:**
> Injecting yourself can seem scary but it doesn't have to be! With a few simple tips, you'll be injecting like a pro in no time. Just remember: you've got this!

**✅ Write this:**
> The first subcutaneous injection takes about 90 seconds from start to finish once you have your supplies lined up. This guide walks through the seven steps in order. The most common mistake new injectors make is rushing step 4 (waiting for the swab to dry before injecting) — skip that and you may sting unnecessarily. Printable cheat sheet at the end of the post.

---

**❌ Don't write this:**
> How to reconstitute BPC-157: First, you'll need some bac water. Then add it to your peptide vial and swirl gently. That's pretty much it!

**✅ Write this:**
> Reconstitution for BPC-157 follows the same math as any lyophilized peptide. If your vial contains 5mg of BPC-157 and you add 2mL of bacteriostatic water, your final concentration is 2.5 mg/mL. A 250 µg dose in a U-100 insulin syringe = 10 units on the barrel. (Use the calculator below to verify your numbers — it shows the visual syringe tick mark.) **This guide is research-context patient-education; BPC-157 is not FDA-approved for human use. Consult your prescriber.** Citations: [reconstitution protocol, year].

## 5. Visual identity

### Colors
- **Primary**: Clinical blue `#2E5A88` (the color of a good nurse's scrubs, not a tech company's logo)
- **Accent**: Warm paper `#F5F1E8` (warmer than clinical white, less dessert than cream)
- **Success**: Moss `#7A9A7E` (used sparingly for "you're done" states and primary CTAs)
- **Caution**: Amber `#C89968` (used for "pause and check" callouts)
- **Alert**: Clinical red `#B74A4A` (used only for true stop-and-call-your-prescriber boxes)
- **Text**: Charcoal `#242424` on paper

Color philosophy: the walls of a well-designed clinic waiting room. Warm but professional. Not sterile. Not consumer.

### Typography
- **Headlines**: **IBM Plex Serif** — trustworthy, readable, slightly institutional
- **Body**: **IBM Plex Sans** — paired with the serif, clean, familiar
- **Calculator outputs + dosage numbers**: **IBM Plex Mono** — numbers are safety-critical, treat them as code
- **Step numbers in procedures**: Sans bold, sized larger than surrounding body

Body at 17px, line-height 1.6. Calculators 18px, monospace at 16px.

### Illustration + imagery style
- **Anatomical line drawings** — not photos of bodies. A clean line drawing of the abdomen, the thigh, the outer upper arm with injection-site zones marked.
- **SVG syringes** — we draw our own syringes as SVG components with tick marks and a highlighted unit mark. The syringe IS the illustration.
- **Step photography, when used** — straight-down flat-lay of supplies (pen, alcohol swab, sharps container) on the neutral paper background. No hands, no bodies, no bandages.
- **Video** — first-person POV (the injector's own view), not third-person instructor shots. Future-phase, not launch.

### What our pages look like
- Single-column, max ~640px for prose
- Step-numbered procedures in a vertical stack with large numbers and clear dividers
- Red/amber/green call-out boxes with a consistent icon language
- Printable PDF link appears at the top of every technique post — not buried at the bottom
- Calculators embedded as real React components with the syringe SVG as the output visualization
- Zero display ads — ever, on this site
- Sparse, precise UI — this is a tool site, not a content site

### What we don't look like
- Medical database site (white, sterile sans, blue-gray, no warmth)
- Patient-advocacy site (rainbow pastels, "journey" language)
- Bodybuilding forum (dark mode, red accents, aggressive typography)
- Wellness blog (warm filters, lifestyle photography, recipe cards)
- Telehealth clinic (CTA-everywhere, "get started" buttons)

## 6. Editorial principles (injectcompass-specific, safety-critical, on top of network)

1. **Step-numbered or not published.** Every technique post has a numbered procedure. Prose walkthroughs are editing stages, not finished pieces.

2. **Opening disclaimer on every technique post.** Identical language: *"This is patient-education material for individuals who have been prescribed injectable medications. It is not a substitute for your prescriber's instructions or the manufacturer package insert."*

3. **Safety call-outs on every complication-possible post.** Red box with "Stop and call your prescriber if…" — specific symptoms, not generic advice.

4. **HowTo schema on every technique post.** Non-negotiable. Rich results are a ranking advantage and a trust signal simultaneously.

5. **Every calculator has unit tests.** Calculator code is safety-critical. Math lives in tested functions, not inline in JSX. CI blocks merges where tests fail.

6. **"Verify with your prescriber" always displayed on calculator output.** Not buried. Next to the output number.

7. **Citations on every technique post.** Published nursing-education literature for technique; manufacturer package inserts for drug-specific. If there's no credible source, we don't publish.

8. **No affiliate links to peptide vendors. Zero. Ever.** Triggers FDA letters. Ends the site.

9. **Credentialed-reviewer byline long-term.** Launch under "The InjectCompass Editorial Team" is acceptable; onboard a named NP or PharmD by month 6 per the research doc. Their review note appears at the top of every technique post after that.

10. **Version the manufacturer-referenced content.** Pen instructions change. FDA label updates happen. Version the post and timestamp the revision. Old version archived, not deleted.

## 9. Reader journey

1. **Reader Googles** "how to inject ozempic" / "peptide calculator" / "bacteriostatic water" / "injection site bruising"
2. **Lands on a technique card, a calculator, or a troubleshooting decision tree** — answer in the first screen
3. **Downloads the printable cheat sheet** (lead magnet, delivered inline, not gated until after the first click)
4. **Subscribes to the newsletter** for "Your First Injection: A One-Page Cheat Sheet" PDF
5. **Returns** for the next question — reconstitution math, travel, a new drug, a new side effect
6. **Cross-visits to PepTips** (for GLP-1 patient context) and **PepVise** (for research context). The three peptide sites are designed to cross-link.
7. **Clicks an affiliate link for medical supplies** (insulin syringes, sharps containers, travel cooler) — high-intent, low-AOV, high-volume
8. **Long-term:** becomes a recurring reference-user. Injection technique is a ~12-month learning curve.

Lead magnet for Wave 1: **"Your First Injection: A One-Page Cheat Sheet"** — printable PDF, 1 page, contains step-numbered procedure + visual syringe + the three questions to ask your prescriber. Delivered on email signup. Free.

## 12. The line we never cross

InjectCompass exists because the physical risk of bad injection technique is real. Every editorial decision has to cost-weight against that risk.

Specifically:
- We do not publish technique content without citations
- We do not recommend specific peptides for human use
- We do not affiliate-link to peptide vendors, compounded or otherwise
- We do not publish calculator outputs without "verify with your prescriber"
- We do not imitate pen manufacturer artwork or pack graphics
- We do not run display ads (ever, on this site)
- We do not let affiliate revenue bias a technique recommendation

The reader is scared and about to do something physical to themselves. The site exists to make that safer, not to monetize their anxiety.
